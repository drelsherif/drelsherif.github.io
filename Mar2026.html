✦ Spotlight · Neurology / Education 2026

Spotlight: Education Research: Bridging the Artificial Intelligence Training Gap

A national survey conducted among Italian neurology residents has highlighted a significant disparity between the extensive use of AI tools and the lack of formal education in AI. Over 40% of residents utilize AI tools weekly, primarily relying on generative AI for clinical decision support. Yet, nearly 80% report that they have received no integrated training on AI as part of their curriculum, and only about one-third consider their AI knowledge to be "good" or better. Despite this educational gap, there is a strong appetite for learning: more than 90% of respondents are eager for structured AI education, with a preference for interactive workshops and hands-on training integrated into their curricula as opposed to passive learning methods. Additionally, concerns about the reliability of AI tools, data privacy, and ethical considerations are prominent, highlighting the urgent need for comprehensive, guided instruction in AI to ensure that residents are not only competent but also confident in their use of these technologies.

Context

AI is already embedded in neurology workflows, particularly neuroimaging and clinical decision support, but education has not kept pace. This gap risks uncritical adoption of tools without sufficient understanding of limitations, bias, or governance—making structured, residency-level AI training increasingly essential.

AI is already here; training is not. If we expect neurologists to use AI responsibly, education must shift from optional exposure to core competency—now..

Stroke Jan. 2026 Moderate

Artificial Intelligence and Novel Trial Designs for Acute Ischemic Stroke: Opportunities and Challenges

This Stroke Treatment Academic Industry Roundtable XIII review outlines how artificial intelligence and novel trial designs—including platform, adaptive, and pragmatic trials—can accelerate acute ischemic stroke research while improving efficiency, enrollment, and real-world relevance. The authors emphasize that AI already plays a critical role in stroke imaging and trial screening, but its future impact depends on high-quality data, transparency, regulatory oversight, and a “human-in-the-loop” model to ensure safety, equity, and clinical validity.

Stroke trials are finally catching up to clinical reality, and AI-enabled platform designs may be the fastest path to answering meaningful questions at scale. The challenge ahead is not technical capability, but disciplined governance—keeping clinicians in control while using AI to move faster without cutting corners.

Scientific Data (Nature Portfolio), January 2026 High

An EEG Dataset for Visual Imagery–Based Brain–Computer Interface

This study introduces a publicly available 32-channel, 1000 Hz EEG dataset from 22 participants performing structured visual imagery tasks across increasing cognitive complexity (geometric figures, animals, and everyday objects), addressing a major limitation in BCI research dominated by motor imagery data. Using deep learning (EEGNet), the authors demonstrate stable and reproducible decoding accuracy up to ~76%, establishing visual imagery as a viable control paradigm for next-generation, non-invasive brain–computer interfaces.

This dataset quietly moves BCI research beyond “move your hand” paradigms toward thought-driven, semantic control, which is far more aligned with real clinical and assistive use. For neurology, this represents a foundational step toward cognitive BCIs that could eventually serve patients with severe motor impairment, aphasia, or disorders of consciousness.

💡 Prompting Tips

Be Specific and Provide Context

The more context and specific detail you give an AI model, the better it can tailor its response to your needs. Vague prompts produce generic, often unhelpful answers.

❌ Bad Example

Tell me about sepsis.

Too vague — the AI doesn't know your role, purpose, or desired output format.

✅ Good Example

Act as a critical care physician. Provide a concise summary of the Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2024 key bundle elements for a nursing education handout. Use bullet points. Keep it under 300 words.

Specifies role, context, format, audience, and length — leading to a much more useful response.

Key Takeaway

Treat AI prompting like writing a clinical order: be precise, include context, and specify your expected output.

🏥 Clinical AI Prompting

The SBARP Framework

A structured approach to clinical AI prompting

▼ click to expand
SBARP is a framework adapted from clinical communication tools to help healthcare professionals craft effective, safe, and context-rich prompts for AI models.
S

Situation

I am working in the ICU and have a 68-year-old patient with acute respiratory failure.

B

Background

Patient has a history of COPD, type 2 diabetes, and CKD stage 3. Currently on 40% FiO2 with an oxygen saturation of 91%.

A

Ask

Summarize the current evidence-based weaning criteria for mechanical ventilation in COPD patients.

R

Role

Act as a critical care pulmonologist with expertise in ventilator management.

P

Parameters

Provide a structured bullet-point summary. Limit to 300 words. Focus on 2024 guidelines. Exclude pediatric data.

📋 Full Prompt Example

Act as a critical care pulmonologist with expertise in ventilator management. Situation: I am working in the ICU with a 68-year-old patient with acute respiratory failure. Background: History of COPD, T2DM, CKD stage 3. Currently on 40% FiO2, SpO2 91%. Ask: Summarize current evidence-based weaning criteria for mechanical ventilation in COPD patients. Parameters: Bullet-point format, max 300 words, 2024 guidelines, adult patients only.

AI Safety Reminders

Clinical AI Safety — This Month's Reminders

As AI tools become more integrated into clinical workflows, these safety principles help protect patients and practitioners.
🔍

Always Verify

AI-generated clinical summaries, drug dosages, and recommendations must be verified against authoritative sources before acting on them.

🔒

Protect PHI

Never enter identifiable patient information (name, DOB, MRN) into public AI tools. Use de-identified data or institution-approved platforms.

⚖️

You Are Accountable

AI is a decision-support tool. The clinician remains fully responsible for all clinical decisions, even those informed by AI output.

🚨

Report Errors

If you notice an AI tool providing dangerous or clearly incorrect clinical information, report it to your informatics or patient safety team.

When in doubt, don't act on AI output alone. Clinical judgment always takes precedence.

🧠 AI Term of the Week

Hallucination

/hə-loo-sə-NAY-shən/

When an AI model generates text that is factually incorrect, fabricated, or not supported by its training data, but presents it confidently as true.

Example in Practice

An AI asked to summarize a patient record confidently includes a medication that was never prescribed.

Why it matters: Understanding hallucination helps clinicians maintain appropriate skepticism and always verify AI-generated content.

📜 AI History 1950

The Turing Test

Alan Turing proposed the "imitation game" — a test of a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from a human. This foundational concept laid the groundwork for artificial intelligence as a discipline.

Historical Impact

The Turing Test sparked decades of philosophical and technical debate about machine intelligence, consciousness, and the benchmarks we use to evaluate AI.

Source: Turing, A.M. (1950). Computing Machinery and Intelligence.
